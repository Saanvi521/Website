Diwali

Diwali is often known as India’s biggest festival and is known for its celebration of light. The light symbolizes protection from the outer darkness; light represents protection whereas darkness represents danger. Diwali is celebrated around late October or early November and is a five-day celebration. Diwali is not only celebrated by Hindus, but also by Jains and Sikhs. In Indian mythology, Lord Rama returned to Ayodhya after defeating Ravana, symbolizing good over evil. Since then, Indians celebrate Diwali by illuminating their houses and offices with lights and Diyas (Indian oil lamp – made of clay).

Holi

When thinking of Holi, colors is the first word that comes to head. Holi is amongst the most popular festivals of India. On this occasion, people get together, forget any resentments, and celebrate their relationships by putting color on each other. The festival is celebrated by lighting up a bonfire during the night, which emphasizes the idea of good over evil. The next day, family and friends unite and show their love for each other by playing with colors and having lots of fun.

Janmastami

Janmashtami celebrates the birth of God Krishna, and is especially prominent in Mathura, because it is the birthplace of Lord Krishna. Most devotees fast until midnight, and then worship Krishna. All families distribute sweets or prasad (food blessed by God) to everyone and celebrate the way Lord Krishna lived his childhood.

Eid

Eid is the most prominent Islam festival in India. On this day, Muslims break their fast (which lasted for a month) and show their gratitude to Allah for always giving them strength and support, which then helped them fast throughout the month of Ramadan. In India, most families celebrate by giving gifts to loved ones and having feasts together. Eid is celebrated on the first day of Shawwal; the tenth month of the Islamic lunar calendar.

Navratri

Hindus celebrate Navratri to honor Goddess Durga for killing Mahishasura. He was a demon known for abusing his powers and was gifted with immortality, with one exception, that only a woman could defeat him. Therefore, Lord Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva (most powerful Hindu gods), came together to make sure the powerful Goddess Durga would put an end to the monster. To celebrate Navratri, some people fast and only eat vegetarian food, whereas Gujaratis are more festive and usually do Garba – a traditional Gujarati style of dance.

Ganesh Chaturthi

Ganesh Chaturthi celebrates the birth of Ganesh, a Hindu God known for wisdom and prosperity as he removes the obstacles of life. The celebration lasts around 10 days and occurs during the Bhadrapada (sixth month of Hindu calendar), which is around mid-August to September. Hindus find it essential to seek the blessings of Lord Ganesh, so that they are guided to the right path and their wishes get fulfilled.

Pongal

Pongal is largely celebrated by the Tamil community of South India. The celebration is meant to show gratitude towards nature – the sun, rain, animals, for their harvest. Pongal is a four-day celebration which also marks the start of “Thai”, a prosperous Tamil month (usually around 14-15 January). The first day is known as Bhogi Pongal, when all the cleaning happens. The second and main day is Surya Pongal, in which the Sun God is celebrated. Next is Maatu Pongal during which the cattle are honored for ploughing land. Lastly, Kaanum Pongal is devoted to strengthening relationships, during which families have feasts together and the youngsters seek blessing from elders.

Vaisakhi

Vaisakhi is one of the most important festivals for the Sikh community of India. In 1699, during which the Khalsa was established – a community of Sikhs that had been baptized, with holy water known as Amrit. To celebrate this occasion, Sikhs worship in Gurudwara (Sikh temple), which is decorated for this festival. There are even parades with people singing hymns from the Guru Granth Sahib – the holy Sikh book. Vaisakhi is usually celebrated on the 13th or 14th of April, and also marks the beginning of Sikh New Year.

Raksha Bandhan

The bond between siblings is a relationship that is highly valued all across the world. In India, this relationship is celebrated through Raksha Bandhan. Raksha Bandhan symbolizes the love between siblings. Raksha Bandhan is celebrated on Purnima, also known as full-moon day, and occurs during Shravan; 5th month of the Hindu calendar (mid-August). During this occasion, sisters tie a Rakhi (bracelet) around the brother’s wrist and wishes for his well-being. In return, the brother gives gifts and promises to protect the sister.

Dussehra

Dussehra celebrates the triumph of Lord Rama after he defeated the Ravana monster, who kidnapped Lord Rama’s wife, Sita. As a result, the festival symbolizes the win of good over evil. Dussehra is celebrated during the 10th day of Ashvina – the seventh month of Hindu calendar (September – October). Dussehra is also on the last day of Navratri and Durga Puja and occurs 20 days before Diwali. Dussehra is celebrated through Ram Leela, which are huge performances around North India that portray this origin story of Dussehra, the victory of Lord Rama, and destruction of Ravana.

Onam

Onam is the biggest festival in Kerala (South India). Similar to Pongal, it is a celebration valuing the harvest season. The festival also honors King Mahabali, because according to a popular legend he brought lots of happiness to the lives of people in Kerala. Onam is celebrated during the beginning of Chingam – the first month of Malayalam calendar (August-September). Onam is a 10-day celebration, with Atham (first day) and Thiruonam (last day) being the most important. The celebration includes feasts, folk music, dances, family games, and elephants, boats, flowers everywhere which showcase Malayalam culture.

Mahavir Jayanti

Mahavir Jayanti is a festival celebrated by the Jain community which marks the birth of Lord Mahavir, the spreader of Jainism. He is honored for his decision of abandoning all his possessions to follow a spiritual path of meditation, truth, non-violence, and spiritual freedom. Jains celebrate Mahavir Jayanti by doing charity, praying, meditating, fasting, following a Satvik diet (vegetarian food without onion or garlic), and visiting Jain temples.
